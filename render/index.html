Welcome to www.SHRDLU -- Beta testing.

SHRDLU is a landmark AI program written by Terry Winograd at MIT, 1968-1971. Though the symbolic approach to language understanding has fallen out of favor since then, few projects since have achieved anything like the language facility of SHRDLU.

Originally written for MACLISP, a long-dead dialect of Lisp, for MIT's Incompatible Time Sharing system (ITS), a long-dead operating system, it was ported to Common Lisp (CLisp to be precise) in the 1990s by a group of students in Missouri. Tom Sgouros, at Brown University, has fixed a few bugs from that port, written a web-compatible graphics front end, and equipped SHRDLU with new digs here on the internet.

Close this window and click the "S" icon below left to get started. Type your commands in the form of English sentences, and end them with a period, question mark or exclamation point. In the process of being ported twice, SHRDLU has lost some of its original functionality. Think of this version as SHRDLU that has suffered a minor stroke. Despite that limitation, SHRDLU can still accommodate requests like "find a block smaller than the one you are holding and put it in the box." But don't take my word for it; give it a try.

If you would like to check out, fix, or otherwise contribute to the code, you'll find a git repo for it at github.

Getting started. SHRDLU's language is deep but not extensive, and the error recovery is not great, either. Try a dialog like the following to see what it can do:

Put the biggest green block in the box.

Ok.

Put the blue block on it.

Ok.

Is there a red block under a green one?

Yes, two of them the large red one and the red cube.

Put the blue block on the large red block.

Ok.

Put the large red block on the red cube.

Ok.

Is there a block smaller than the blue pyramid?

Yes, the red cube.

Does it support a block?

Yes, the large red one.

You can find renderings of historical SHRDLU dialogs at the links above.
